Slip and Fall in Ontario: How the Occupiers' Liability Act Affects Your Claim

Slip and fall accidents in Ontario are governed by the Occupiers' Liability Act — a law that places a legal duty of care on property owners. Understanding your rights before you approach an insurer or a property owner can make a significant difference.

7 min read Ontario Accident Review

Every year, thousands of Ontarians are injured in slip and fall accidents on someone else's property — in parking lots, grocery stores, on icy sidewalks, in apartment buildings, and at public facilities. Many of those people don't pursue a claim because they assume it's their own fault, that nothing can be done, or that the process is too complicated.

In many of these situations, the property owner or occupier has a legal obligation to maintain the premises in a reasonably safe condition — and when they fail to do so, they may be liable for the injuries that result. Ontario's Occupiers' Liability Act defines this duty of care.

Who Is an "Occupier" Under Ontario Law?

The Occupiers' Liability Act doesn't just apply to property owners. An "occupier" is anyone who has physical possession of a property or responsibility for its condition. This can include:

  • Property owners (residential and commercial)
  • Tenants and leaseholders in control of the premises
  • Businesses operating from leased space
  • Municipalities and public agencies (for sidewalks, parks, public buildings)
  • Contractors managing or maintaining a property

In some situations, multiple parties may share occupier status — for example, a building owner and a commercial tenant may both be considered occupiers of different parts of the same property.

The Duty of Care Under the Occupiers' Liability Act

Section 3 of the Occupiers' Liability Act states that an occupier owes a duty to take such care as in all the circumstances is reasonable to see that persons entering the premises, and the property brought on the premises, are reasonably safe while on the premises.

What this means in practice: The occupier doesn't have to make their property perfectly safe — they have to take reasonable steps under the circumstances. What's "reasonable" depends on factors like the type of property, who is expected to use it, the nature of the hazard, and how quickly the occupier could have become aware of the problem.

This reasonableness standard is where most slip and fall cases turn. An occupier who fails to salt an icy walkway after a known overnight freeze, or who ignores a wet floor spill during business hours, may be found to have breached their duty of care.

What You Need to Prove in a Slip and Fall Claim

To succeed in a slip and fall claim under the Occupiers' Liability Act, you generally need to establish:

  • The hazard existed. There was a dangerous condition on the property (ice, a wet floor, an uneven surface, poor lighting, etc.).
  • The occupier knew or should have known. The occupier either had actual knowledge of the hazard or should have discovered it through reasonable inspection.
  • The occupier failed to act reasonably. Despite knowing (or being expected to know) about the hazard, the occupier failed to fix it or provide adequate warning.
  • The hazard caused your injury. There is a direct connection between the unsafe condition and the harm you suffered.
  • You suffered actual damages. Your injury resulted in measurable harm — medical expenses, lost income, pain and suffering.

The occupier may raise defences, including that the hazard was obvious, that you contributed to the accident through your own negligence (contributory negligence), or that they took reasonable steps to address the hazard.

Municipal Claims: Special Rules Apply

If your slip and fall occurred on a public sidewalk, municipal road, or other government-maintained property, there are additional procedural requirements that apply. Under the Municipal Act and related regulations, you are generally required to provide written notice of your claim to the municipality within 10 days of the accident. Failing to do this can permanently bar your claim against the municipality — regardless of how strong your case might otherwise be.

Important: The 10-day municipal notice requirement is one of the shortest deadlines in Ontario's personal injury system. If your fall occurred on a public sidewalk, road, or municipal property, do not wait.

Documenting Your Slip and Fall

Evidence is critical in slip and fall claims. The hazard that caused your injury may be cleaned up, repaired, or otherwise disappear within hours or days. Useful evidence includes:

  • Photographs of the hazard taken at the scene, as soon as possible after the accident
  • Witness contact information from anyone who saw the fall or the hazard
  • Incident reports filed with the property owner, manager, or store
  • Medical records documenting your injuries from as early as possible
  • Records of weather conditions (for outdoor falls involving ice or snow)
  • Security footage, if available — this must be requested promptly before it is overwritten

Limitation Period: 2 Years

Under Ontario's Limitations Act, the general limitation period for bringing a slip and fall claim is 2 years from the date of the accident. If you were a minor at the time of the accident, the limitation period may be tolled (paused) until you turn 18. In most cases, however, there is no exception — if you don't commence an action within 2 years, your claim is statute-barred.

What To Do After a Slip and Fall in Ontario

  • Report the incident to the property owner, manager, or municipality as soon as possible
  • Document everything — photos, witnesses, your injuries, the conditions
  • Seek medical attention promptly, even if you feel the injury is minor
  • If the fall occurred on municipal property, consider whether the 10-day notice requirement applies
  • Get a review of your situation before deciding whether to pursue a claim
